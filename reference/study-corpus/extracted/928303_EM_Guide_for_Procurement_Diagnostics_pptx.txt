A clearly defined set of guidelines for recognition of real financial savings versus operational value should be established What counts as savings Spend permanently removed from budgets : If demand is eliminated or sourcing reduces cost and Finance updates the baseline, it’s real savings Negotiated price or scope reductions captured at the line-item level:  Savings must show up directly on contracts, POs, or invoices – not just as estimates. It still counts even if the money is later reused elsewhere Clear P&L impact : There must be a direct link from the action, contract outcome, and net income improvement Reinvested savings only count if formally approved:  If “savings” are spent elsewhere, they don’t count unless Finance signs off on a documented ROI case What is value creation, but not direct savings Better intake, triage, and request handling:  Faster work, fewer handoffs, less rework Standardized contracts and renewals:  Easier execution, fewer errors, less firefighting More efficient supplier and transaction operations:  Less effort in onboarding, PO processing, and invoice resolution Clear governance and spend visibility:  Faster decisions through dashboards and escalation paths

A procurement diagnostic is run to assess the operating model and  quantify the value at stake  Large waves of vendor renewals Upcoming renewals with  high-spend vendors Cost pressure / margin scrutiny Procurement perceived as slow or reactive to business line needs and upcoming renewals Limited vendor negotiation strategies Typical triggers An end-to-end procurement and sourcing policy rewrite A procurement technology implementation A compliance or audit review of vendors or contracts What the  diagnostic is not
Effective procurement diagnostics  independently track operational assessment and   value   capture , then reconnect the two Two core lenses Value at stake Current baseline spend analysis Current spend vs. industry benchmarks  Contracts (e.g., SKUs, services, pricing, terms) Operating effectiveness Current S2C process  vs. best in class Governance and coordination between sourcing and other teams (e.g., legal, risk, cyber, business lines) Enablers (e.g., category mgmt., tech and data, KPIs, org. and governance, talent building) Key questions  to answer How much value is realistically available for capture through procurement, given the current spend profile and market conditions? Where does value leak across the end-to-end procurement lifecycle today? Why is procurement unable to capture this value consistently? To what extent are challenges driven by gaps in category management, fragmented ways of working, or non-standardized negotiation and sourcing strategies? What capability, process, governance, data, or incentive constraints are limiting sustained value capture? What must change to enable and sustain procurement impact on a go-forward basis?

Effective spend baselining is required to assess gap to peers Addressable  spend definition Common pitfalls Accepting raw GLs without normalization (i.e., vendor consolidation, category standardization, etc.) Mixing addressable and non-addressable spend Letting scope creep into accounts payable (AP) or compliance Third-party spend in scope informed by clear category taxonomy at the L1, L2 areas  (incl. tech (cloud, software, hardware), professional services,  travel, etc.) Clear exclusions should be removed from the addressable spend baseline (e.g., taxes, royalties, etc.)
