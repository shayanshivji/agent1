Rapid expansion of diagnostics used for disease monitoring
Disease monitoring based on Minimal Residual Disease (MRD) already established in 
ALL and MM, expanding to further indications incl. CLL – MRD enables more accurate 
prognosis and improved treatment management
Slower expansion of diagnostics used for treatment selection
Fewer cDx-enabled biomarkers linked to MoA targets than in solid tumors, currently limited to 
specific indications (CML and AML) - e.g., BCR-ABL, IDH2 
Majority of testing done to detect prognostic biomarkers (e.g., (Del)17p/TP53), i.e. biomarkers 
which are not directly linked to therapy MoA targets – unlike for HER2, ALK, etc. in solid tumors

Published studies have identified economic rationale 
in support of NGS
Haslem et. al. 
2017
Patients who received NGS testing and targeted therapy had better outcomesthan those who did not receive 
NGS and associated targeted therapies.  Costs per week of PFS were similar for the two groupswhether the 
patient received NGS or not, but outcomes (PFS) were improved.
Reitsma et.al. 
2019
An analysis of 20 patients who enrolled in phase I clinical trials following NGS testing showed that clinical trial 
enrollment may have resulted in an estimated $25,000 per-patient potential cost savings accrued to the 
payer, by diversion of drug costs to the trial sponsor.
Pennel et.al. 
2019
Upfront NGS testing for all relevant molecular targets in NSCLC is less costly than sequential / exclusionary 
single-gene testing strategies. Upfront NGS testing also results in the largest number of patients with targetable 
genetic alterations being identified in the shortest period of time.
Stuten et.al. 
2019
NGS is expected to be moderately cost effectivecompared with single marker genomic testing in patients with 
aNSCLC.  Efforts to increase the proportion of patients who receive targeted therapies would improve the cost 
effectiveness of NGS, assuming that incremental costs and outcomes of targeted treatments remain unchanged
